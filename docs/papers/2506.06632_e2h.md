# E2H Reasoner: Curriculum Reinforcement Learning from Easy to Hard Tasks Improves LLM Reasoning

> **Authors**: Shubham Parashar, Shurui Gui, Xiner Li, Hongyi Ling, Sushil Vemuri, Blake Olson, Eric Li, Yu Zhang, James Caverlee, Dileep Kalathil, Shuiwang Ji (Texas A&M University, divelab).
> **Version read**: arXiv 2506.06632v? (ICLR 2026 paper). Versions: v1 2025-06-07; check arXiv abs for current version at re-read.
> **Links**: [arxiv abs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.06632) ; [alphaxiv](https://www.alphaxiv.org/abs/2506.06632) ; [code](https://github.com/divelab/E2H-Reasoning) ; [wayback abs](https://web.archive.org/web/2026*/https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.06632) ; local PDF (not yet captured; should live at `../raw/papers/2026-05-06_e2h.pdf`).

## TL;DR

- **Vanilla GRPO fails on hard reasoning tasks at 1.5B to 3B model scales** because the reward signal is too sparse to bootstrap on. Easy tasks alone are not enough either; the model must transition.
- **E2H is a data-side curriculum**: partition tasks by difficulty (zero-shot pass-rate or hop count), then schedule sampling probability from easy to hard over training using either cosine or Gaussian distributions.
- **Fade-out is essential**: easy tasks must be reduced over time, otherwise they dominate and block hard-task learning. This is the core operational insight.
- **Stack with GRPO**: E2H is drop-in (no algorithmic change to GRPO; only the data sampler changes). One line in the dataloader.
- **Best config**: E2H-Gaussian with `β = 0.5` (progression speed) and `σ = 0.5` (concentration). Beats balanced sampling by 5 to 23 pp across Blocksworld / Countdown / MATH on Qwen 1.5B.
- **Theoretical backing**: finite-sample policy-iteration analysis showing curriculum reduces total sample requirement.

## Problem

Vanilla RL on hard reasoning tasks is **ineffective for small models** (1.5B to 3B) because rewards are sparse: the model rarely produces correct rollouts, so advantage estimates are zero across most groups, and gradient signal collapses. Standard remedies (longer training, more rollouts) hit compute walls before they help. The paper asks: can a curriculum let small models build up to hard tasks the same way humans do?

## Method

### Data partition

Tasks are bucketed by difficulty into K levels (k = 0 easiest to k = K-1 hardest). Difficulty proxy varies by domain:
- **Math / Countdown**: zero-shot base-model pass-rate (well-defined when the model is the policy being trained).
- **Blocksworld**: plan length (number of moves needed).
- **Multi-hop QA (our extension)**: hop count (NQ = 1, HotpotQA = 2, MuSiQue = 2 to 4).

### Schedules

**E2H-Cosine (E2H-C)**: cosine interpolation between difficulty levels.

```
S_cosine(t, k) = α_t · (K - k - 1) + (1 - α_t) · k
α_t            = 0.5 · (1 + cos(π · t / T))
```

`α_t` decays from 1 (all easy) to 0 (all hard) over T training steps. Mid-training is the linear-mix region.

**E2H-Gaussian (E2H-G)**: Gaussian mixture with progression speed `β` and concentration `σ`.

```
S_Gaussian(t, k) = exp(- (x_t - μ_k)² / (2 σ²))
x_t              = (t / T)^β · (K - 1)
μ_k              = k - 1
```

The "active" difficulty `x_t` walks from 0 to K-1 along a power-law curve `(t/T)^β`. `σ` controls how sharply the sampler concentrates on the active level vs. its neighbours.

### Algorithm: GRPO unchanged

E2H does **not** modify GRPO itself. It only changes which examples the dataloader returns at each step. The KL term, reward, advantage, and update rule are all standard.

## Training setup

(From paper experimental section + the divelab/E2H-Reasoning repo.)

- **Base models tested**: Qwen 1.5B (primary), with additional sweeps on other small models per the paper.
- **GPUs**: not stated explicitly in our reading; the repo's training scripts target multi-GPU but don't pin a count.
- **Algorithm**: GRPO with `num_generations = 8`, KL coefficient `β = 0.001`, learning rate `1e-6`, cosine LR schedule, bf16, seed 42.
- **Curriculum hyperparameters**: `curriculum_schedule = "gaussian"`, `beta_scheduler = 0.5`, `sigma = 0.5`.
- **Total steps**: not stated in our reading; verify on re-read.

## Data

- **Train domains** (closed-book): MATH, Countdown (arithmetic puzzle), Blocksworld (planning).
- **Eval splits**: hard-split accuracy (top difficulty bucket) on each domain. Some experiments also report easy / medium splits.
- **Important**: the paper does **not** evaluate on retrieval-augmented multi-hop QA. Applying E2H to NQ → HotpotQA → MuSiQue is a novel extension by this thesis.

## Results

(Paper headline numbers; Qwen 1.5B; hard-split accuracy.)

| Method | Blocksworld Hard | Countdown Hard | MATH Hard |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| GRPO (balanced sampling) | 21.1% | 18.1% | 46.3% |
| E2H-G best | **32.9%** | **41.0%** | **48.7%** |
| Δ | +11.8 pp | +22.9 pp | +2.4 pp |

Largest gains where the easy-to-hard gap is largest (Blocksworld plan-length curriculum, Countdown by pass-rate). The MATH gain is smaller because the easy-to-hard structure is less crisp on MATH.

## Takeaways for us

- **Drop-in for our recipe**: E2H is the data-scheduler half of the recipe stack proposed in [docs/report/SUPERVISOR_MEETING_2026-05-07.md § 6](../report/SUPERVISOR_MEETING_2026-05-07.md). Stack it on a Search-R1 GRPO baseline; no GRPO code change.
- **Difficulty mapping is clean for our 7-benchmark suite**: NQ (1-hop) = ordinal 0, HotpotQA (2-hop) = ordinal 1, MuSiQue (2 to 4 hop) = ordinal 2. Hop count is the proxy; no zero-shot pass-rate estimation needed because EM v1 numbers already order them (0.390 / 0.263 / 0.055 base). See [docs/research/DATASET_ANALYSIS.md](../research/DATASET_ANALYSIS.md).
- **Use Gaussian schedule with paper's defaults**: `β = 0.5, σ = 0.5`. The cosine schedule is simpler but the Gaussian gives slightly better gains in their experiments and is what their best config uses.
- **Fade is non-negotiable**: a stage-based version (fixed 300 steps per stage as we sketched in the supervisor brief) is a reasonable first approximation but the continuous schedule is the paper's preferred mechanism. The mistake to avoid is keeping NQ at fixed weight throughout training; doing so collapses to vanilla GRPO with extra easy-task overhead.
- **Novelty caveat**: paper uses closed-book math/planning/coding. Applying it to retrieval-augmented QA with live search calls in the GRPO rollout is a thesis novelty. The mechanism should transfer (cold-start bootstrapping, progressive difficulty), but the convergence rates and the pp gain may differ.
- **JustRL counter-evidence applies**: per [arxiv 2512.16649](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.16649), curriculum stacks can hurt OOD performance. The recipe must be validated against a plain-GRPO C-minimal control; if C-minimal beats the stack on val EM, the stack is hurting.

## Limitations

- **Small-model focus**: paper validates on 1.5B. Effect at 2B (our setting) and beyond is extrapolated, not measured.
- **Closed-book domains**: no retrieval interaction in the rollout; their tokens-per-step is much lower than ours. The cost of E2H scheduling is invisible (no extra rollout length); for us, scheduling MuSiQue (longer chains) up the easy-to-hard ladder will increase per-step time as training progresses, which complicates wall-clock budgeting.
- **Difficulty proxy is domain-specific**: the zero-shot pass-rate proxy used for MATH does not exist for retrieval QA without an extra eval pass. We use hop count instead, which may not align perfectly with model-perceived difficulty.
- **Compute claim is light**: paper does not state total wall-clock or GPU type explicitly in our reading. Treat reproduction-cost claims with caution.

## Open questions / followups

- Does E2H help on retrieval QA the same way it helps on closed-book math? (Phase-2 ablation #5 in [docs/TODO_2026-05-04.md](../TODO_2026-05-04.md).)
- Should the cosine or Gaussian schedule be preferred for our setting? Default to Gaussian per paper, but cheaper to test cosine first if we can only afford one ablation.
- How long should each curriculum stage last? Paper sets `T` = total steps; our 1005-step horizon may be too short for Gaussian to fully transition. Worth checking the schedule curve at our T before launching.
- Is fade rate per-stage-300-steps (our supervisor brief proposal) close enough to the continuous Gaussian schedule that we get most of the gain without the implementation complexity?
